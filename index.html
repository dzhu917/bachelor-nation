Bachelor Nation: Visualized

If you've been on social media or watch TV, you've probably heard of ABC's Bachelor franchise. Every season, one person is crowned the Bachelor or Bachelorette, and a group of contestants vie for their love by going on dates throughout the season. Contestants are eliminated every week at rose ceremonies, during which the Bachelor(ette) grants a rose to contestants chosen to move forward.


The franchise has run for a while — the Bachelor has 21 seasons, and the Bachelorette has 13. With hundreds of contestants who have gone through this unconventional process of finding love, we wondered what a visualization of the franchise might look like. Who tends to win the Bachelor(ette)'s heart? Do first impression roses mean anything? We dove into the data behind the franchise, its contestants, and how it all pans out!

Contestant Elimination

Contestants are eliminated each week, as the Bachelor/Bachelorette develops deeper relationships. We've indicated elimination week through color here: the lighter the color of the dot, the earlier the contestant was eliminated. Winners, therefore, are represented by the darkest dot in each cluster.

Trickle-Down Elimination

Here, contestants are sorted based on elimination week — as you can see, contestants are eliminated week by week until only one contestant remains. This winner gets engaged to the Bachelor(ette).

First impression roses as an indicator of success

In the first week of each season, one contestant received a "First Impression Rose".

Out of all Bachelor and Bachelorette seasons, 26 first impression roses were given out. Of the contestants who received a first impression rose, 7 (27%) of them would go on to win the show. In contrast, only 26 out of the 860 (3%) other contestants that have been on the show ultimately won their season

Runner-ups vs. Winners

In each season, only one contestant can be crowned a winner (i.e. get engaged to the Bachelor(ette). The show usually depicts the Bachelor(ette)'s choice between the final two contestants as heart-wrenching and nearly impossible, because of how deep both relationships have become.

If the runner-ups and winners are really so difficult to choose between, how do their paths differ from each other? Is there any way to predict who will win and who will come in second place?

For each season, we're focusing only on winners and runner-ups: winner on top, runner-up on bottom.
